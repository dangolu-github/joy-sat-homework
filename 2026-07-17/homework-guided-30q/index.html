Guided Homework — Inferences + Command of Evidence

Joy · Class 13 · 30 questions

How to use this guided homework
  • Try the full passage and choices first.
  • If you are stuck, open Check the hint. The hint shortens the conditions and marks words to test; it does not give an answer.
  • For Questions 1-15, use only the method reached in class: Evidence Chain — facts → repeated subject/variable → smallest supported conclusion.
  • For Questions 16-30, review Claim Lock — exact claim → needed evidence → direct-fit test.
  • For every option, explain whether its key words preserve the target, condition, direction, comparison, and strength.

Question 1

Moderate

Botanists studying a shrub common in fire-prone grasslands collected seeds from several populations. The researchers stored all seeds under the same conditions and provided equal moisture during germination tests. Untreated seeds rarely germinated, and brief exposure to heat alone produced little change. Seeds exposed to chemicals found in smoke, however, germinated at much higher rates even when they had not been heated. In natural grasslands, smoke can spread beyond the patches that burn most intensely. The findings suggest that ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. heat prevents the shrub's seeds from germinating after every grassland fire.
  2. B. smoke-related chemical cues may trigger germination even for some seeds outside the hottest burned patches.
  3. C. all shrubs in fire-prone habitats use identical germination cues.
  4. D. the shrub's seeds can germinate only after being physically damaged.
Check the hint

Use the hint to simplify—not to guess. Keep every condition, then test all four options.

Short skeleton: same seeds/moisture; heat alone -> little change; smoke chemicals -> much more germination; smoke travels beyond hottest patches -> ?

Logic chain: Connect the effective cue to the places that cue can reach. Keep may/some; do not turn one shrub into all plants.

Option word check:

  • A — match/trap words to test: heat, prevents, every fire — compare the direction and strength with 'little change.'
  • B — match/trap words to test: smoke-related cues, may, some seeds, outside hottest patches — check cue, certainty, and location.
  • C — match/trap words to test: all shrubs, identical cues — check whether the study included other shrub species.
  • D — match/trap words to test: only, physically damaged — check whether damage was tested or required.

Question 3

Moderate

Researchers placed temperature sensors in summer roosts used by the same bat species and tracked hundreds of tagged bats for six weeks. Bats in cooler caves generally began their evening flights later than bats in warmer caves. On unusually warm days, however, bats from the cooler caves emerged earlier than they usually did, while sunset time changed very little across the study period. Cave entrances were similar in size, and researchers observed no consistent difference in nearby insect abundance. These observations most strongly suggest that ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. roost temperature may help influence when the bats begin their evening activity.
  2. B. sunset time has no effect on the behavior of any bat species.
  3. C. bats choose cooler caves because those caves contain more food.
  4. D. every bat in a warm cave leaves at exactly the same time.
Check the hint

Use the hint to simplify—not to guess. Keep every condition, then test all four options.

Short skeleton: cooler roost -> later flights; warmer day in same cooler roost -> earlier flights; sunset nearly stable; food/entrances similar -> ?

Logic chain: The repeated variable is roost temperature and the measured outcome is emergence time. Use may help influence, not sole cause.

Option word check:

Question 16

Moderate

Ornithologist Rina Patel argues that juvenile storm petrels use the low-frequency calls of adult birds to locate nesting crevices when dense coastal fog limits visibility. Patel notes that the calls travel around rock faces better than higher-frequency sounds do, but she needs behavioral evidence that the calls actually guide juveniles toward potential nest sites.

Which finding, if true, would most directly support Patel's argument?

  1. A. Juvenile storm petrels have proportionally larger eyes than adult storm petrels do.
  2. B. Fog density at the study cliffs was similar during the first and second halves of the nesting season.
  3. C. Adult storm petrels called more frequently on clear nights than on foggy nights.
  4. D. In paired field trials during low visibility, juveniles approached artificial crevices broadcasting adult calls sooner and more often than otherwise identical silent crevices.
Check the hint

Use the hint to simplify—not to guess. Keep every condition, then test all four options.

Short skeleton: Claim: adult low-frequency calls -> help juveniles locate crevices when fog limits sight. Needed evidence: calls present vs absent + juvenile approach behavior + fog/low visibility.

Logic chain: Lock the mechanism, condition, behavior, and comparison before judging whether a finding directly pays for the claim.

Option word check:

  • A — match/trap words to test: larger eyes — same animal, but test whether this measures use of calls or locating crevices.
  • B — match/trap words to test: similar fog across season — check whether a stable condition shows the proposed behavior.
  • C — match/trap words to test: adults call more on clear nights — compare condition and measured subject with the claim.
  • D — match/trap words to test: paired trials, low visibility, calls vs silent, approached sooner/more — check every Claim Lock part.

Question 17

Difficult

Economic historian Elena Rojas claims that an 1846 reduction in tolls on the North Canal, rather than the construction of grain warehouses along the canal in the same year, was chiefly responsible for the subsequent increase in inland wheat shipments. Which finding, if true, would most directly weaken Rojas's claim?
