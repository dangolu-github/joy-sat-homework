Inferences: Make Only the Leap the Text Pays For

Joy · Class 13 · Reading Mini-Unit

Today’s focus:
  • turn a dense passage into a short evidence chain;
  • reject an answer that is possible but not supported;
  • explain why the closest distractor crosses the text boundary.

Evidence and inference are the same contract

In Command of Evidence, you asked: Which evidence directly pays for this claim? In Inferences, you ask the reverse: What is the strongest conclusion this evidence can pay for?

Evidence → smallest earned conclusion

If an answer needs one extra fact, one extra cause, one extra group, or one stronger word, it has spent evidence the passage never gave you.

Method 1 — Evidence Chain

Recognition cue

The passage gives several facts, findings, or stages, and the answer must connect them.

  1. Turn each important fact into a short bullet or arrow.
  2. Circle the repeated subject, measured variable, or comparison group.
  3. Connect only the arrows stated by the passage.
  4. Say the smallest conclusion in your own words before reading the choices.

P1 + P2 (+ P3) → one small leap

Example

A museum kept its lights dim to protect old textiles. After the museum installed filters that blocked damaging wavelengths, the textiles showed no additional fading even though visitors could see them under brighter light.

Evidence chain: damaging wavelengths blocked → brighter visible light → no added fading.

Earned inference: the filters may allow brighter display without increasing the measured fading. The text does not prove that all light is harmless or that every textile will react identically.

Closest traps: a related fact with no arrow; a conclusion that skips a condition; a causal claim when the passage shows only an association; an absolute claim from a limited result.

Question 1 · Guided application

Moderate

Marine biologists grew similar-sized fragments from several coral colonies in two nurseries. Some donor colonies had survived an unusually warm summer, whereas others had experienced only typical temperatures. Before testing, all fragments received the same light and nutrients. When the nursery water was later warmed, fragments from the heat-exposed donors retained more photosynthetic algae than fragments from the other donors did. Because the fragments had been raised under the same nursery conditions before the test, the result suggests that ______

Evidence chain: donor survived heat → fragment raised under same conditions → fragment retained more algae during later warming. Boundary: the result supports a possible retained advantage, not guaranteed survival in every environment.

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. nursery conditions have no effect on how coral fragments respond to heat.
  2. B. some heat-tolerance advantage associated with the donor colonies may persist in their fragments.
  3. C. every coral colony exposed to warm water will permanently retain all of its photosynthetic algae.
  4. D. fragments from heat-exposed colonies grow faster than all other coral fragments do.

Keep the chain short, preserve every condition, and stop at the smallest conclusion the evidence earns.
