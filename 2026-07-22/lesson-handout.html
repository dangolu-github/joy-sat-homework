Words in Context: Make the Blank Sign a Contract

Joy · Class 15 · Craft and Structure

Today’s target

A Words in Context question is not mainly asking, “Do I know four definitions?” It is asking, “Which option performs the exact job this passage requires?”

By the end of class, you should be able to state and use three methods without notes:

  1. Context Contract — compress the passage, mark the logic clue, and predict the blank’s job before looking at choices.
  2. Precision Ladder — test meaning, tone, degree, and sentence role; “almost right” is wrong.
  3. Second-Meaning / Unknown-Word Protocol — treat unfamiliarity as neutral and make context, not familiarity, decide.

7-minute bridge: compress before you interpret

A city archive lists workshops only if they operated inside the old fortified district and still have surviving tax records. No glass workshop appears in the list before 1810.

Condition chain: listed → inside district and surviving records. No listing before 1810 → no confirmed listed workshop under those conditions.

What the evidence does not prove: that no glass workshop existed anywhere in the city before 1810.

Use the same compression today: preserve the condition, direction, time, and degree before choosing a word.

Method 1 — Context Contract

Recognition cue: “Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word or phrase?”

  1. Compress: reduce the passage to the claim or contrast around the blank.
  2. Anchor: mark a restatement, contrast, cause, example, limitation, or evaluation clue.
  3. Predict: write a rough target such as “limited,” “still uncertain,” or “supports but does not prove.”
  4. Compare: open the choices and ask which one signs that exact contract.

Trap: reading only the words beside the blank or choosing an option that repeats the topic but reverses the passage’s logic.

Example

Because early photographs preserve outlines but flatten many color differences, the conservator treats them as limited records of the painted rooms.

Contract: useful for shape + unreliable for color → “useful but incomplete,” not “false” and not “comprehensive.”

Method 2 — Precision Ladder

Recognition cue: two or more choices seem broadly related to the passage.

Climb the ladder in order:

  1. Meaning: does the core definition match?
  2. Tone: is the passage approving, critical, cautious, or neutral?
  3. Degree: does the word preserve “slight,” “some,” “may,” or “not fully” instead of exaggerating?
  4. Sentence role: does it describe the correct object, action, or judgment?

Trap: accepting a synonym that matches the topic but is too strong, too positive, too negative, or aimed at the wrong thing.

Example

The revised budget makes the project feasible, but the tight schedule still makes it difficult.

Precision check: feasible means possible. “Effortless” is positive in the wrong degree because the second clause explicitly preserves difficulty.

Historian Amara Bell praises a newly digitized collection of merchant letters for revealing how traders discussed risk in private. Bell also notes that most surviving letters were written by wealthy firms, while the correspondence of small-scale traders was rarely preserved. She therefore uses the collection to revise several claims about commercial decision-making but cautions against treating it as representative of all merchants. Bell’s assessment of the collection is best described as ______.

Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word or phrase?

  1. celebratory
  2. dismissive
  3. indifferent
  4. qualified

Meaning: useful evidence, with a stated limit.

Tone: neither pure praise nor rejection.

Degree: the limit narrows the claim; it does not erase the collection’s value.

Method 3 — Second-Meaning / Unknown-Word Protocol

Recognition cue: a very ordinary option looks strange, or one unfamiliar option remains after the others only partly fit.

  1. Reset the familiar word: ask what meaning it must have here, not what meaning comes to mind first.
  2. Keep unknowns neutral: unfamiliar does not mean “advanced and correct,” and it does not mean “wrong.”
  3. Eliminate known failures: reject any known choice that breaks meaning, tone, degree, or role.
  4. Verify by contract: the survivor must fit every clue, not merely sound sophisticated.

Trap: familiarity bias—choosing a known word that only partly works—or prestige bias—choosing an unknown word because it looks impressive.

Example

The heated resin remains plastic long enough to be shaped into the mold.

Here plastic means “capable of being shaped,” not “made of synthetic material.” The sentence, not the first dictionary meaning you remember, controls the choice.

Question 5 · Guided application

Moderate

A reviewer calls the new survey of coastal dialects unusually detailed and praises its inclusion of recordings from communities often omitted from earlier research. The reviewer nevertheless observes that all interviews were conducted during one season, when many younger residents were away for work. This limitation does not invalidate the survey, but it should ______ enthusiasm for claims that the recordings capture the full range of local speech.

Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word or phrase?

  1. vindicate
  2. intensify
  3. temper
  4. nullify

First meaning that may distract: temperature.

Needed meaning: reduce or moderate, without eliminating.

Strength lock: “does not invalidate … but” rules out both full support and total destruction.

Exit routine — say it without notes

  1. What is the blank’s contract?
  2. Which option is the closest trap?
  3. Which lock does that trap break: meaning, tone, degree, or role?
  4. If one word is unfamiliar, what do you do—and what do you refuse to do?
