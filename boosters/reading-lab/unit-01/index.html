Reading 1 · Modeled

Flexibility can help—but it has limits

Research context

Adapted and synthesized from a 2017 U.S. Geological Survey report and research summary by Erik Beever and colleagues.

Open the original USGS source ↗

When environmental conditions change, an animal population may shift its range, undergo genetic adaptation across generations, or acclimate within the lifetimes of individual animals. Behavioral flexibility—the capacity to alter actions as conditions change—can contribute to that third response. Because behavior can change more rapidly than inherited traits, it may temporarily buffer a population from some effects of climate variability.

To examine this possibility, researchers reviewed 186 studies involving behavioral responses to climatic conditions. Changes in the timing of activities such as migration, breeding, and egg laying were especially common. Temperature was identified as the most likely driver of behavioral change in 67 percent of the studies; precipitation, food supply, habitat, humidity, and wind appeared less frequently. American pikas offered one example: some populations adjusted foraging, habitat use, or heat-regulating postures in complex rocky environments.

The researchers nevertheless resisted treating flexibility as a complete solution. Changing one activity can reduce the time or energy available for another, and every species must still perform behaviors necessary for survival and reproduction. Flexibility may therefore increase tolerance only up to a point. The review supports the value of behavioral change while leaving open a crucial question: under increasingly extreme conditions, when will behavioral adjustment cease to be enough?

Claim

Behavioral change can rapidly buffer some animals from environmental change.

Evidence

Across 186 studies, behavior shifted with climate variables; temperature was most frequently identified.

Limit

Flexibility has costs and may help only up to a point; it is not guaranteed to prevent decline.

Reading 2 · Guided

Camera traps challenge fixed schedules

Research context

Adapted and synthesized from a 2025 U.S. Geological Survey Climate Adaptation Science Centers report on a global camera-trap study.

Open the original USGS source ↗

Ecologists have traditionally described mammal activity with fixed labels: diurnal species are active during the day, nocturnal species at night, crepuscular species near twilight, and cathemeral species at several times. A global camera-trap study suggests that these categories often conceal substantial variation. More than 200 researchers assembled 8.9 million observations from over 20,000 camera-trap sites in 38 countries, covering 445 mammal species across six continents.

Only 39 percent of the species fit neatly into one conventional activity category, while 74 percent regularly shifted their schedules in response to environmental conditions or human activity. Elk, for instance, were active during both day and night where human presence was low but became primarily nocturnal where it was high. Comparable schedule changes appeared in gray foxes, snowshoe hares, striped skunks, and porcupines.

The study therefore treats flexible timing as pervasive rather than exceptional. Yet the management implication is not simply that animals can adapt without consequence. If human activity pushes a species away from the hours when food, shelter, or mates are most accessible, a changed schedule may carry costs. Knowing when animals alter their activity could help managers limit human disturbance during especially important periods.

Reading 3 · Independent

What exactly counts as flexibility?

Research context

Synthesized from Lea, Chow, Leaver, and McLaren (2020), an open-access article licensed CC BY 4.0.

Open the original research article ↗

The term behavioral flexibility appears across animal-cognition research, but researchers do not always use it in the same way. Sometimes it refers broadly to behavior that is not fully constrained by instinct. Elsewhere it describes differences among species in innovative foraging, rapid adjustment within an individual, or variation among populations facing different environments. Lea and colleagues argue that this range of meanings creates a measurement problem: two studies may claim to examine flexibility while testing different traits.

To make the concept more precise, the authors separate an animal's knowledge about possible responses from its observable choice. Their model represents the animal as having different response strengths—roughly, estimates of how promising each action is. A flexibility parameter then determines how consistently those strengths translate into behavior. An animal with lower flexibility may repeatedly choose the response with the highest current strength; a more flexible animal may sample alternatives more readily even when their estimated values are close.

Simulations showed that adjusting this parameter could reproduce some behavioral patterns previously attributed to flexibility. The authors do not claim, however, that a single parameter explains every form of adaptive variation. They instead offer a testable distinction: observed changes in behavior may reflect changes in what an animal has learned, changes in how it converts knowledge into action, or both. Empirical work is still needed to determine when these components vary together.
