Question 1 · Central Ideas

Difficult

Behavioral ecologists distinguish inherited adaptation, which unfolds across generations, from behavioral flexibility, which can alter an animal's response within its lifetime. A review of 186 studies found frequent changes in migration, breeding, and foraging as climatic conditions shifted. Such flexibility can temporarily buffer a population because individuals can draw on a broader behavioral repertoire before genetic change occurs. Yet every adjustment consumes time or energy that cannot be used elsewhere, and no behavioral response can make an organism indefinitely tolerant of extreme conditions. Flexibility is therefore a contingent advantage: potentially rapid, but neither cost-free nor unlimited.

Which choice best states the main idea of the text?

  1. A. Inherited adaptation generally enables animal populations to respond to climate change more quickly than behavioral flexibility does.
  2. B. Changes in migration and breeding are the only behavioral responses that can protect animals from extreme conditions.
  3. C. The review establishes that populations with broad behavioral repertoires can survive any degree of environmental change.
  4. D. Behavioral flexibility can provide a rapid but limited response to changing environmental conditions.

Question 2 · Command of Evidence

Moderate

In reviewing 186 studies of animal responses to climatic conditions, Erik Beever and colleagues recorded the environmental factor that each study identified as most likely to have prompted a behavioral change. Temperature was identified in 67 percent of the studies. Precipitation, food availability, habitat conditions, humidity, and wind were also identified, but each appeared less often. A researcher citing the review claims that temperature was the most consistently documented climatic influence on the behaviors examined.

Which finding from the review most directly supports the researcher's claim?

  1. A. The review considered studies of migration, breeding, foraging, and other behaviors.
  2. B. Temperature was identified as the likely driver in 67 percent of the studies, a larger share than for any other listed factor.
  3. C. Food availability and habitat conditions were among the factors identified by some studies.
  4. D. The review included 186 studies conducted by different research teams.

Question 3 · Inferences

Difficult

American pikas are sensitive to prolonged heat, but populations living in complex rocky terrain can vary when and where they forage. Some retreat into cool crevices during the hottest hours; others forage near dawn or adopt postures that reduce heat exposure. These adjustments may attenuate short-term thermal stress. However, pika declines have still been observed at sites where extreme heat persists and suitable cool spaces are scarce.

Which choice most logically follows from the text?

  1. A. Behavioral adjustments may reduce pikas' exposure to heat without eliminating the risks created by sustained extreme temperatures.
  2. B. Pikas that forage near dawn are more likely to survive than pikas that retreat into cool crevices.
  3. C. The availability of complex rocky terrain guarantees that a pika population will not decline.
  4. D. Pika declines occur only when the animals fail to alter their foraging behavior.

Question 4 · Text Structure and Purpose

Difficult

When conditions change, shifting the timing of one activity can protect an animal from an immediate threat. Elk, for example, may become more active at night where daytime human presence is high. But the same shift can constrain access to food or mates that are most available during daylight. Ecologists therefore evaluate flexible behavior not only by whether it reduces one pressure but also by whether it produces a consequential trade-off.

Which choice best describes the function of the underlined sentence in the text as a whole?

  1. A. It presents evidence that elk cannot alter their activity schedules in response to people.
  2. B. It identifies the environmental condition that originally caused elk to become nocturnal.
  3. C. It qualifies the preceding example by identifying a possible cost of the behavioral response.
  4. D. It argues that access to food and mates is unrelated to the timing of animal activity.

Question 5 · Central Ideas

Difficult

Ecologists often classify mammals as diurnal, nocturnal, crepuscular, or cathemeral. Yet a global study assembled 8.9 million camera-trap observations of 445 species under heterogeneous environmental conditions. Only 39 percent of the species fit neatly into one conventional category, while 74 percent regularly shifted their active hours in response to local conditions or human presence. The researchers argue that fixed labels can obscure flexibility that matters for conservation: managers may need to know not merely whether a species is active, but when disturbance pushes its activity away from the hours most favorable for feeding or reproduction.

Which choice best states the main idea of the text?

  1. A. Camera traps are valuable primarily because they allow ecologists to classify mammals into four conventional categories.
  2. B. A large dataset reveals widespread schedule flexibility that fixed activity labels may miss, with consequences for conservation planning.
  3. C. Human presence causes nearly all mammal species to become exclusively nocturnal regardless of local conditions.
  4. D. Conservation managers should protect feeding periods but do not need to consider reproduction or other activities.

Question 6 · Quantitative Evidence

Moderate

A global camera-trap study examined whether conventional labels adequately represent mammal activity. The researchers reported four aggregate features of the dataset: 8.9 million observations, more than 20,000 monitoring sites, 445 mammal species, and regular schedule shifts in 74 percent of the species. They concluded that many mammals do not maintain one fixed activity pattern across conditions.

Which finding from the study most directly supports the researchers' conclusion?

  1. A. The dataset contained 8.9 million observations.
  2. B. Cameras were placed at more than 20,000 sites.
  3. C. The analysis included 445 mammal species.
  4. D. Seventy-four percent of the species regularly shifted their activity schedules.

Question 7 · Inferences

Difficult

In areas with little human presence, elk were active during both day and night; in heavily visited areas, they became primarily nocturnal. This temporal displacement can reduce encounters with people, but plant quality, visibility, temperature, and access to mates also vary across the twenty-four-hour cycle. Consequently, an activity shift that solves one problem may expose the animals to different constraints.

Which choice most logically follows from the text?

  1. A. Nocturnal elk necessarily obtain higher-quality food than elk active during the day.
  2. B. Human presence is the only environmental condition capable of altering elk activity.
  3. C. Avoiding people at one time of day may carry costs because other resources and conditions also vary by time.
  4. D. Elk in lightly visited areas are unable to change their activity schedules.

Question 8 · Text Structure and Purpose

Difficult

Labels such as diurnal and nocturnal can be useful heuristics: they summarize the periods when a species is commonly active. In the global camera-trap dataset, however, only 39 percent of species fit neatly into one category, and nearly three quarters shifted their schedules across conditions. The researchers retain the labels as broad descriptions but caution against treating them as invariant biological properties.

Which choice best describes the function of the sentence presenting the 39 percent and nearly three-quarters findings?

  1. A. It supplies empirical evidence for the researchers' limitation of fixed activity labels.
  2. B. It demonstrates that the four activity labels were originally defined incorrectly.
  3. C. It explains why researchers excluded most mammal species from the study.
  4. D. It establishes that schedule shifts are always caused by human activity.

Question 9 · Text Structure and Purpose

Difficult

Animal-cognition researchers use behavioral flexibility to refer to several different phenomena, including innovative foraging, adjustment within an individual, and variation among populations. Lea and colleagues argue that this breadth makes studies difficult to compare. To delineate one component more precisely, they propose a model that separates an animal's estimates of possible actions from the process by which those estimates become observable choices. The model is not presented as a complete theory of adaptation; it is a way to generate testable predictions about one source of behavioral variation.

Which choice best describes the main purpose of the text?

  1. A. To demonstrate that innovative foraging is the only valid form of behavioral flexibility
  2. B. To compare the adaptive success of animal populations in several environments
  3. C. To identify ambiguity in a research concept and explain a model intended to clarify one component of it
  4. D. To argue that mathematical models should replace observations of animal behavior

Question 10 · Model Application

Difficult

In Lea and colleagues' model, an animal assigns a response strength to each available action based on what it has learned. A separate flexibility parameter determines how consistently the animal selects the currently highest-strength response. At one end, choice is nearly invariant; at the other, selection is more stochastic, so alternatives are sampled even when their estimated values are slightly lower. The distinction allows researchers to ask whether two animals differ in learning, in response selection, or in both.

Which hypothetical finding would most directly illustrate a difference in response selection rather than in learning?

  1. A. Two animals assign similar strengths to the same actions, but one repeatedly selects the top-ranked action while the other samples several alternatives.
  2. B. Two animals encounter different foods and therefore learn about different sets of possible actions.
  3. C. One animal revises an action's strength after receiving new information about its reward.
  4. D. One animal has more opportunities than another to observe members of its group.

Question 11 · Inferences

Difficult

A change in observable behavior can arise because an animal has learned that an action's value is different, because the animal has changed how consistently it converts existing estimates into choices, or because both processes changed. Lea and colleagues' model separates these components conceptually, but field observations often record actions without directly measuring the estimates that preceded them. Researchers must therefore be cautious when using behavior alone to disentangle learning from response selection.

Which choice most logically follows from the text?

  1. A. Field observations cannot provide any useful evidence about animal behavior.
  2. B. A change in an animal's choice always shows that the animal learned a new action.
  3. C. Learning and response selection necessarily change together in every environment.
  4. D. Observing a behavioral change may not by itself reveal which underlying process produced it.
