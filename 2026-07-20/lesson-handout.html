Inferences: Make Only the Leap the Text Pays For

Joy · Class 14 continuation · Inferences

By the end of class, you should be able to:
  • turn a dense passage into a short evidence chain;
  • identify the exact job of the blank before reading the options;
  • reject an answer that is possible but not supported;
  • explain why the closest distractor crosses the text boundary.

10-minute bridge: evidence and inference are the same contract

In Command of Evidence, you asked: Which evidence directly pays for this claim? In Inferences, you ask the reverse: What is the strongest conclusion this evidence can pay for?

Evidence → smallest earned conclusion

If an answer needs one extra fact, one extra cause, one extra group, or one stronger word, it has spent evidence the passage never gave you.

Method 1 — Evidence Chain

Recognition cue

The passage gives several facts, findings, or stages, and the answer must connect them.

  1. Turn each important fact into a short bullet or arrow.
  2. Circle the repeated subject, measured variable, or comparison group.
  3. Connect only the arrows stated by the passage.
  4. Say the smallest conclusion in your own words before reading the choices.

P1 + P2 (+ P3) → one small leap

Example

A museum kept its lights dim to protect old textiles. After the museum installed filters that blocked damaging wavelengths, the textiles showed no additional fading even though visitors could see them under brighter light.

Evidence chain: damaging wavelengths blocked → brighter visible light → no added fading.

Earned inference: the filters may allow brighter display without increasing the measured fading. The text does not prove that all light is harmless or that every textile will react identically.

Closest traps: a related fact with no arrow; a conclusion that skips a condition; a causal claim when the passage shows only an association; an absolute claim from a limited result.

Question 1 · Guided application

Moderate

Marine biologists grew similar-sized fragments from several coral colonies in two nurseries. Some donor colonies had survived an unusually warm summer, whereas others had experienced only typical temperatures. Before testing, all fragments received the same light and nutrients. When the nursery water was later warmed, fragments from the heat-exposed donors retained more photosynthetic algae than fragments from the other donors did. Because the fragments had been raised under the same nursery conditions before the test, the result suggests that ______

Evidence chain: donor survived heat → fragment raised under same conditions → fragment retained more algae during later warming. Boundary: the result supports a possible retained advantage, not guaranteed survival in every environment.

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. nursery conditions have no effect on how coral fragments respond to heat.
  2. B. some heat-tolerance advantage associated with the donor colonies may persist in their fragments.
  3. C. every coral colony exposed to warm water will permanently retain all of its photosynthetic algae.
  4. D. fragments from heat-exposed colonies grow faster than all other coral fragments do.

Question 2 · Unaided transfer

Difficult

No map this time. Build the chain yourself.

Economic historians studying a nineteenth-century port found few bank records showing loans to dockworkers. Personal letters from the same period, however, frequently mention small advances made by shopkeepers, relatives, and mutual-aid societies. Several letters also discuss repayment dates and requests for extra time. These arrangements often relied on verbal promises and were not entered in formal ledgers, and no complete register of informal lenders survives. The historians therefore caution that the scarcity of bank-loan records ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. proves that dockworkers preferred gifts to every form of borrowing.
  2. B. shows that mutual-aid societies made larger loans than banks did.
  3. C. indicates that shopkeepers kept more detailed financial records than banks did.
  4. D. may understate how often dockworkers obtained credit through informal channels.

Method 2 — Blank Job

Recognition cue

The information may be clear, but the blank can still require the wrong kind of idea if you ignore the transition, punctuation, or stem.

  1. Read the stem and the words immediately before the blank.
  2. Name the blank's job: conclusion, support, contrast, limitation, example, or shared synthesis.
  3. Predict the direction of the answer in plain English.
  4. Reject any choice that has the right topic but performs the wrong job.
SignalLikely blank job
therefore, thus, suggests, henceconclusion earned by earlier evidence
because, since, due tosupport or reason for a claim
however, although, yetcontrast, qualification, or exception
for example, specifically, colonexample, detail, or explanation of the prior idea
either explanation, in both casesshared conclusion, not a choice between the two

Example

Several bird species reached the island after a storm. However, only species able to eat the island's hard seeds established lasting populations. ______

The blank's job is not “another fact about the storm.” It must preserve the contrast: arrival was possible for several species, but lasting establishment depended on food compatibility.

Question 3 · Guided application

Moderate

Urban ecologist Noura Bell compared moth activity near newly installed amber streetlights with activity near older white streetlights of similar brightness and height. Teams sampled paired sites on the same nights and for the same amount of time. The total number of moths recorded was similar at the two kinds of lights, but the species composition differed: several short-wavelength-sensitive species appeared less often near the amber lights. Bell identified one immediate implication of the result: ______

Blank job: the colon asks for the named implication. Prediction: amber lighting changed which species appeared, not necessarily the total number of moths.

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. changing light color may alter which moth species are present even when overall moth activity remains similar.
  2. B. amber streetlights eliminate all effects of artificial lighting on insects.
  3. C. cities with white streetlights contain more moths than rural areas do.
  4. D. short-wavelength-sensitive moths are unable to see amber light under any conditions.

Difficult

A public inscription from an ancient trading city begins with a decree addressed to local residents. The stone also contains a translation in a language used by visiting merchants, and its final lines specify how foreign traders could appeal a customs decision. The translated section uses standard commercial terms found on documents from several neighboring ports, suggesting that nonlocal readers could follow its procedures. Although the decree's opening names a local audience, ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. local residents were probably unable to understand any part of the decree.
  2. B. visiting merchants must have written the original version of the decree.
  3. C. the inscription was likely designed to communicate with at least some nonlocal readers as well.
  4. D. foreign traders opposed every customs policy described in the inscription.

Method 3 — Scope & Strength Lock

Recognition cue

Two choices may fit the topic, but one quietly changes how much the evidence proves.

  1. Underline limits: some, may, tended to, in this sample, under X condition, by date Y.
  2. Lock the group, condition, time, variable, direction, and certainty.
  3. Run the “extra noun / extra verb / extra strength” test on each choice.
  4. For the closest distractor, finish: “This would require evidence that ______.”
Text givesDo not upgrade to
some / may / associated withall / will / caused by
one sample or locationthe whole population or region
effect under condition Xeffect under every condition
uncertainty about claim Aproof that the opposite of A is true
silence or absence in one recordproof that the event or attitude never existed
Four high-frequency unsupported leaps: correlation → cause; sample → population; may → must; evidence is limited → opposite claim is proven.

Question 5 · Guided application

Difficult

To test a method for preserving wooden artifacts, conservators cut samples from the same type of aged timber and placed half in cases that reduced both humidity changes and exposure to airborne pollutants. The other samples remained in standard cases in the same building. After one year, evaluators who did not know which case held each sample recorded less cracking and less surface discoloration in the protected group. Because the new cases changed two environmental factors at the same time, the study most strongly suggests that ______

Scope lock: two variables moved together. Boundary: the treatment package helped, but this design cannot identify which component produced which effect.

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. airborne pollutants are the sole cause of cracking in all wooden artifacts.
  2. B. the combined case treatment improved preservation, but the individual contribution of humidity control and pollutant reduction remains uncertain.
  3. C. humidity changes affect discoloration but can never affect cracking.
  4. D. standard cases accelerate damage at the same rate in every climate.

Question 6 · Unaided transfer

Difficult

State the exact population and condition before choosing.

A study of twelve artificial ponds in a dense city found that ponds bordered by native flowering plants contained more dragonfly species than ponds bordered by closely cut grass. Researchers surveyed each pond for the same number of weeks and found the pattern even after comparing ponds of similar size. They nevertheless note that city ponds are warmer, more isolated from one another, and exposed to more artificial light than many ponds in rural landscapes. The study therefore supports the conclusion that ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. native plant borders may be associated with greater dragonfly diversity in the urban ponds studied, but the result may not generalize unchanged to rural ponds.
  2. B. native plants increase dragonfly diversity in every freshwater habitat.
  3. C. artificial light is the only factor limiting dragonfly diversity in cities.
  4. D. rural ponds with grass borders contain no dragonfly species.

Final Transfer — Prove the Method Without Hints

For each item, write four tiny notes before choosing: chain · blank job · scope lock · closest trap.

Question 7

Moderate

Researchers analyzing textile fragments from an ancient cemetery found a costly purple dye on several garments from high-status burials but not on fragments from lower-status burials at the same site. Chemical tests confirmed that the purple color was not a result of soil staining. The surviving sample is small, however, and ordinary clothing from nearby settlements is poorly preserved; researchers also could not determine whether the dyed cloth was made locally or imported. The evidence most reasonably suggests that ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. people of lower status were legally forbidden to wear any colored clothing.
  2. B. every high-status person in the region wore purple garments daily.
  3. C. purple dye was produced only for burial rituals.
  4. D. in the cemetery sample, the costly dye was associated with higher-status burials, though its wider use remains uncertain.

Question 8

Difficult

During a two-year drought, ecologists monitored several bird populations across neighboring valleys. Species that regularly ate seeds, insects, and fruit declined less than species that depended mainly on one seasonal fruit. Surveys confirmed that the broad-diet species switched foods as the seasonal fruit became scarce. Rainfall, predator abundance, and nesting habitat were similar across the monitored sites, though the researchers did not manipulate the birds' diets experimentally. The pattern suggests that ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. drought never affects birds that eat more than one kind of food.
  2. B. the seasonal fruit disappeared because broad-diet birds consumed all of it.
  3. C. access to several food types may have helped buffer some bird populations against the drought's effects.
  4. D. predator abundance is generally unrelated to bird population change.

Question 9

Difficult

Two climate models using the same regional emissions forecast predict earlier spring snowmelt in a mountain basin. One model attributes the change mainly to warmer air temperatures; the other gives a larger role to dust that darkens the snow and increases absorption of sunlight. The models differ in the amount of change predicted for individual slopes. Although they disagree about the dominant mechanism, both predict that peak streamflow will occur earlier in the year. Thus, either model implies that ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. dust has no effect on snowmelt when air temperatures rise.
  2. B. both warmer air and dust must contribute equally to every year of early snowmelt.
  3. C. annual precipitation in the basin will necessarily increase.
  4. D. seasonal water availability downstream may shift earlier even if the precise cause of faster snowmelt remains disputed.

Question 10

Difficult

A novelist's early travel diaries rarely describe conversations with local guides, leading some critics to assume that the writer ignored their knowledge. Yet contracts preserved in the writer's archive list several guides as paid research assistants, and later essays thank them for identifying regional stories and place-names. The diaries were kept primarily as brief records of routes and expenses; meals, lodging, and weather are also reduced to abbreviations rather than described narratively. These facts suggest that the diaries' silence ______

Which choice most logically completes the text?

  1. A. proves that the guides refused to share information during the journeys.
  2. B. may reflect the documents' limited purpose rather than the absence of meaningful collaboration.
  3. C. shows that the later essays were written by the guides instead of the novelist.
  4. D. indicates that route planning was the guides' only contribution.

Class 13 takeaway: the correct inference is not the most interesting possibility. It is the strongest statement that every relevant detail can support without borrowing evidence.
