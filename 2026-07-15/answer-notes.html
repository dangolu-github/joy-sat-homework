Today’s target

Claim → required evidence → direction and conditions → direct fit

Diagnosis gate

Classify the preview error only after hearing the explanation: careless (misread word/number), repeated weakness (same trap family), knowledge gap (cannot state claim/evidence link), or speed/strategy issue (read choices before locking the claim). A single error earns a brief repair; it does not redirect the whole class.

The full evidence route

  1. Circle the task word: illustrate, support, weaken, or complete.
  2. Lock the claim: identify the variables, direction, comparison, and conditions.
  3. Predict the evidence: state what a successful finding or quotation must show.
  4. Test direct fit: accurate and relevant are necessary, but the answer must also do the exact job.
Why this route works

B07 requires claim restatement and evidence prediction before choices; B02 likewise asks students to paraphrase the argument and anticipate the needed information. This prevents the choices from defining the task. [B07, PDF pp. 128-139; book pp. 133-144. B02, PDF pp. 194-203.]

Method 1 — Claim Lock

Recognition cue: the passage contains a hypothesis, conclusion, assertion, or interpretation that later evidence must address.

Compress before you compare

Rewrite the claim as a small logical structure. Keep every condition that could change the result.

When condition C is present, X should produce more / less Y than the comparison.

Example: A historian claims that adding late-evening train service increased theater attendance specifically among workers whose shifts ended after 9 p.m.

Claim Lock: late service → higher theater attendance for post-9 p.m. workers.

Trap: Overall theater attendance rose after the timetable changed.
Relevant topic, but it loses the worker group and cannot isolate the claim.
Direct fit: Attendance rose among post-9 p.m. workers at stations receiving late service but not at similar stations without it.
Target group, comparison, and direction all match.
Teacher explanation

Write the claim as variables rather than a summary: service timing (X), attendance (Y), late-shift workers (condition), comparison stations (control). A claim is not locked until every element that could distinguish the correct answer is visible. [B07, PDF pp. 128-139; B02, PDF pp. 194-203.]

Chinese note: 不要只说“这个答案跟剧院有关”。要把人群、条件、方向和比较对象全部保留下来；少一个条件就可能是 half-right trap。

Question 1 — Guided application

Moderate

Agricultural historian Mina Okafor argues that farmers in a semiarid valley adopted deep-rooted sorghum because it made harvests more stable during years of unusually low rainfall than the previously dominant grain did.

Which finding, if true, would most directly support Okafor’s argument?

  1. A. In high-rainfall years, both grains produced larger harvests than they did in low-rainfall years.
  2. B. During low-rainfall years, farms growing sorghum had fewer crop failures and less variation in yield than comparable farms growing the earlier grain.
  3. C. Archaeologists found that sorghum seeds from the valley were larger than wild sorghum seeds from a neighboring region.
  4. D. Several centuries after sorghum became common, farmers in the valley constructed new irrigation channels.
Claim Lock: group/condition __________ · predicted direction __________ · comparison __________
Answer B · L12-COE-01

Core skeleton: low rainfall + sorghum → more stable harvests than earlier grain. B supplies the target condition, the outcome (stability), and the comparison. A is true-looking but about high rainfall and both grains. C concerns domestication, not stability. D is later and cannot explain the original adoption.

SAT trap: topic relevance without causal/claim alignment. Chinese note: B 不是因为提到 sorghum，而是因为同时满足低降雨、稳定性和旧作物比较三个条件。

[Original question; pattern and method calibrated against B07, PDF pp. 128-139, and B02, PDF pp. 194-203.]

Question 2 — Unprompted transfer

Difficult

A media scholar claims that an 1890s newspaper’s price reduction expanded its rural readership more than its urban readership. The scholar notes that the lower price made the paper affordable to more readers but argues that the publisher’s new rail-delivery agreements were especially important outside cities.

Which finding, if true, would most directly weaken the scholar’s claim?

  1. A. The paper added agricultural reports after reducing its price.
  2. B. Some rural readers shared one copy among several households.
  3. C. Rail companies delivered the paper to rural towns earlier in the day than they had before the agreements.
  4. D. Subscription records show a large increase in urban districts after the price reduction but almost no change in rural districts served by the new rail agreements.
Write one sentence: The claim predicts __________, but the weakening evidence shows __________.
Answer D · L12-COE-02

Core skeleton: claim predicts rural increase > urban increase. D shows urban increase > rural increase, the direct opposite. A is rural-themed but lacks readership evidence. B may make subscriptions underestimate readers, so it could protect rather than weaken the claim. C supports the proposed rural-delivery mechanism.

SAT trap: confusing relevant rural details with evidence that reverses the predicted comparison. Chinese note: weaken 不是找负面信息，而是找和原预测方向最冲突的信息。

[Original question; support/undermine logic calibrated against B07, PDF pp. 128-139; POE extension B06, PDF pp. 336-370.]

Method 2 — Direct-Fit Test

Recognition cue: two or more options sound connected to the topic, especially when one repeats words from the passage.

Four ways a tempting answer fails

  1. Wrong target: it discusses the topic but not the exact claim.
  2. Wrong direction: it supports when the question asks to weaken, or reverses the comparison.
  3. Missing condition: it proves only half of a two-part claim.
  4. Extra bridge: it works only after adding an assumption that the passage never gives.

Strictness test: If the explanation depends on “maybe,” “could mean,” or several unstated steps, the evidence is probably not direct enough.

Example: Claim: a reading lamp improves proofreading accuracy only in low light. Direct evidence must compare lamp versus no lamp in low light and show fewer errors; a survey saying that readers like the lamp is relevant but does not test accuracy.

Teacher explanation

B06 calls out half-right traps; B07 and the PPT2 textual route emphasize closest/direct support rather than thematic similarity. Make Joy label each rejected option with one short failure phrase. [B06, PDF pp. 336-370. Teacher synthesis checked against B07, PDF pp. 128-139, and B02, PDF pp. 194-203.]

Chinese note: “可能可以解释成……”通常说明中间补了题目没有给的桥梁。最强证据应该不需要脑补。

Question 3 — Guided application

Moderate

Biologist Sora Lin hypothesizes that the eyespots near the fragile tips of a moth’s wings protect the moth by directing bat attacks away from the moth’s body.

Which finding, if true, would most directly support Lin’s hypothesis?

  1. A. In flight-tunnel trials, bats struck the removable wing tips of eyespot moths more often than their bodies, while attacks on otherwise similar moths without eyespots were concentrated near the body.
  2. B. Moths with eyespots were more common in forests with many bat species than in forests with few bat species.
  3. C. The pigments that form the eyespots also occur on the moths’ legs.
  4. D. Bats took slightly longer to begin hunting when the tunnel lights were dimmed.
Closest trap: ______ · exact failure: wrong target / wrong direction / missing condition / extra bridge
Answer A · L12-COE-03

A directly tests both parts: eyespots change attack location, and that location shifts from the body to expendable wing tips relative to a no-eyespot comparison. B is correlation without the claimed redirection mechanism. C is unrelated anatomy. D changes light but not eyespots or attack location.

SAT trap: B is plausible ecological context but needs an extra bridge from forest distribution to protection mechanism. Chinese note: 题目不是问 eyespot 是否和 bats 同时出现，而是问它是否把攻击从身体转移到翅尖。

[Original question; method calibrated against B07, PDF pp. 128-139, and B02, PDF pp. 194-203.]

Question 4 — Unprompted transfer

Difficult

In a fictional 1912 novel, a factory clerk visits a newly electrified district. A critic claims that the narrator presents technological progress as unsettling rather than purely triumphant.

Which quotation from the novel most effectively illustrates the critic’s claim?

  1. A. “The lamps rose in a perfect row, brighter than any festival lanterns he remembered.”
  2. B. “Shopkeepers remained at their counters long after sunset, grateful for the extra hour of trade.”
  3. C. “The street shone like noon, yet every window seemed to watch him, and the cables overhead hummed with a life he could neither name nor trust.”
  4. D. “Children gathered beneath the nearest lamp to finish their lessons before supper.”
State the two ideas the quotation must contain: progress = __________ and emotional effect = __________.
Answer C · L12-COE-04

C contains both components: visible technological change (electric light/cables) and unease (watched, unnamed, distrusted). A, B, and D show brightness or benefits but not the required unsettling response. A is the closest trap because grandeur can feel intense, but the quotation supplies no discomfort.

SAT trap: missing the second half of a two-part literary interpretation. Chinese note: 看到 progress 相关词还不够；必须同时找到 unsettling 的情绪证据。

[Original fictional quotation set; illustrate-claim pattern calibrated against B07, PDF pp. 128-139, and B02, PDF pp. 194-203.]

Method 3 — Data Lock

Recognition cue: a graph or table appears, and several choices may accurately report a number.

Read less data, more precisely

  1. Read the title, labels, units, groups, and time points.
  2. Lock the claim before searching the data.
  3. Trace only the rows, columns, or series named by the claim.
  4. Run two checks: Is the statement true? and Does it prove the claim?

Example: If a claim says Neighborhood P had the largest percentage increase in ridership from 2020 to 2025, compare the two years and calculate change relative to the 2020 value. The largest 2025 total may be accurate but still fail the claim.

True-but-useless trap: a choice reports an accurate number from the table but addresses the wrong group, year, comparison, or outcome.
Direct data fit: every number is accurate and the comparison has the direction and conditions required by the claim.
Teacher explanation

B07 warns that a student can interpret a chart correctly and still miss the question because multiple choices report accurate data. B02 adds axis/scale, group, unit, correlation-direction, and evidence-only checks. [B07, PDF pp. 140-153; book pp. 145-158. B02, PDF pp. 170-193.]

Chinese note: 图表题不是“找一个正确数字”，而是“找一个能完成指定论证的正确数字”。先锁定论证，再去取数。

Question 5 — Guided application

Moderate

A restoration ecologist predicts that wetland plots with at least 70% native plant cover will support more amphibian species than plots below that threshold.

PlotNative plant coverAmphibian species observed
North74%9
East68%5
South81%11
West43%4

Which choice best describes data from the table that support the ecologist’s prediction?

  1. A. The East plot had 68% native plant cover and five amphibian species.
  2. B. The North and South plots both exceeded 70% native plant cover and had more amphibian species than either plot below 70%.
  3. C. The South plot had two more amphibian species than the North plot.
  4. D. The West plot had less native plant cover than the East plot.
Threshold/group __________ · outcome __________ · comparison __________
Answer B · L12-COE-05

B tests both groups around the 70% threshold and the claimed outcome. A is accurate but gives only one below-threshold point. C compares two above-threshold plots, so it cannot test the threshold claim. D compares plant cover but omits amphibian species.

SAT trap: true statement that omits the required cross-threshold comparison or outcome. Chinese note: 题目要求验证 70% 分界线，必须同时看线上的两组和线下的两组，并比较物种数。

[Original table and question; pattern calibrated against B07, PDF pp. 140-153, and B02, PDF pp. 170-193.]

Question 6 — Unprompted transfer

Difficult

A management researcher hypothesizes that remote teams with at least four overlapping work hours per day collaborate more efficiently than teams with less overlap.

TeamDaily overlapCross-member messages per projectMedian days to resolve shared tasks
A2 hours465.1
B3 hours514.8
C5 hours425.4
D6 hours395.7

Which choice best describes data from the table that weaken the researcher’s hypothesis?

  1. A. Team B had five more cross-member messages per project than Team A.
  2. B. Team A resolved shared tasks in a median of 5.1 days, while Team B did so in 4.8 days.
  3. C. Teams C and D had at least four overlapping work hours per day.
  4. D. Teams C and D had fewer cross-member messages and took longer to resolve shared tasks than Teams A and B.
The hypothesis predicts __________. The ≥4-hour teams actually show __________.
Answer D · L12-COE-06

The claim predicts that C/D (≥4 hours) should have stronger collaboration outcomes than A/B. D reports the opposite on both provided indicators: fewer cross-member messages and longer resolution time. A and B compare only the low-overlap teams. C identifies the target group but provides no outcome.

SAT trap: selecting an accurate group label without data that address efficiency. Chinese note: weaken 题先写出预测方向，再找和预测相反的完整数据。D 同时在两个指标上反向。
